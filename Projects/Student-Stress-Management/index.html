Student Stress Management Research

Human-Centered Design research on improving student well-being at MSOE

UX Research User Interviews Affinity Mapping
School Project - UXD 1001 Human-Centered Design at MSOE

Project Context

In UXD 1001 Human-Centered Design at the Milwaukee School of Engineering, my two-person team embarked on a design sprint focused on improving how MSOE students manage stress during busy academic weeks. The project came at a particularly relevant time, as many students were struggling to find balance between their coursework demands and personal well-being. Our goal was to understand the root causes of student stress and identify opportunities for meaningful intervention.

My Role: User Research Lead

My primary responsibility was to conduct student interviews that would help define the pain points we could address in our final project. I took ownership of the interview process, from developing interview questions to synthesizing findings into actionable insights. This required me to approach each conversation with empathy and curiosity, listening carefully to understand not just what students said, but the underlying patterns in their experiences.

Research Methodology

I conducted three in-depth interviews with MSOE students, each focusing on their daily routines, stress triggers, and coping mechanisms. The interviews were semi-structured, allowing me to explore interesting threads while ensuring I covered all necessary topics. During each conversation, I asked open-ended questions like "Walk me through a typical busy week" and "What happens when you start feeling overwhelmed?" to uncover authentic insights rather than leading participants toward predetermined conclusions.

As I conducted the interviews, I took detailed notes, paying attention to both explicit statements and implicit behaviors. After completing all three interviews, I began the critical work of finding patterns and consistencies across the different student experiences. This analysis revealed common themes that would become the foundation of our problem statement.

Synthesis and Analysis

To make sense of the wealth of qualitative data I had gathered, I organized my findings into an affinity map. This process involved writing individual observations on sticky notes and then grouping them by theme and relationship. Patterns began to emerge around phone usage, sleep habits, time management, and distraction during peak productivity hours.

Working with my teammate, I helped build a profile for a typical student who fit our identified pain points. We gave this persona a name, background, and specific challenges that represented the composite of our interview subjects. This persona would serve as a touchstone throughout the rest of the design process, helping us stay focused on real user needs rather than assumptions.

Key Findings

  • Phone usage was the core issue: Students consistently reported that their phones were their biggest source of distraction and a major contributor to poor sleep habits.
  • Morning and evening phone habits: Students frequently reached for their phones immediately upon waking and before bed, setting a negative tone for their days and disrupting sleep quality.
  • Distraction during peak hours: Phone notifications and the temptation to check social media created significant disruptions during classes and homework sessions.
  • Awareness without action: Most students recognized their phone habits were problematic but struggled to implement lasting changes on their own.

Problem Statement and "How Might We"

After synthesizing our research findings, my team narrowed the problem enough to draft our "How Might We" statement, a crucial tool in human-centered design that frames challenges as opportunities. Our statement focused on helping students reduce phone usage during critical times—before bed, immediately after waking, and during peak academic hours—to improve their productivity and overall well-being.

This framing gave us clear direction for the Ideate and Prototype phases of the project. Rather than trying to solve the broad problem of "student stress," we now had specific moments in the day where intervention could have meaningful impact. The research phase had successfully transformed a vague concern into a focused design challenge.

Skills Developed

This project significantly strengthened my user research skills, particularly in conducting qualitative interviews and synthesizing findings into actionable insights. I learned how to create a comfortable environment where interview subjects felt safe sharing honest perspectives about their struggles. The affinity mapping process taught me how to identify patterns in qualitative data and translate them into design opportunities.

Beyond the technical skills, I developed a deeper appreciation for the empathy required in human-centered design. The research phase isn't about confirming your own assumptions—it's about genuinely listening to users and letting their experiences guide the design process. This mindset shift has influenced how I approach problem-solving in all my subsequent projects.

Impact and Next Steps

Our research provided a solid foundation for the remainder of the design sprint. The clear problem statement and user persona gave the team confidence that we were addressing real needs rather than hypothetical problems. The insights about phone usage timing proved particularly valuable, as they suggested specific intervention points where a solution could have maximum impact.

Moving into the Ideate and Prototype phases, we had a clear target: create a solution that helps students establish healthier phone habits during critical times of the day. The research phase had done its job— transforming uncertainty into clarity and giving us the direction we needed to design something truly useful.
